# Human Malate Dehydrogenase, MDH2
# Uniprot: P40926
# P133L variation


## Description of the protein and variation site
The P133L variant in the human MDH2 protein is a swap that results in the replacement of a proline with a leucine at position 133. Position 133 is located within a Rossman fold, which is a part of the protein that defines structure and holds the NAD+ that is needed for the enzyme to function properly. ₁ In the wild type of MDH2 that proline is very important because of its rigid shape and which creates a specific shape that stabilizes local loops. This acts as a structural anchor that keeps all local loops in the right place. These loops are what help the active site be able to bind with things like malate or oxaloacetate.

When looking at the 3D structure you can see that position 133 is not buried right within the active site but it provides support for the whole protein. When the structures are aligned, the models are only slightly shifted and align well in some places more than others. When you change the proline to a leucine that is a more flexible amino acid, the protein shifts shape so slightly that the active site does not line up perfectly. Because of this, the enzyme can do its job well which is why people that have this mutation end up with severe health issues like encephalopathy. ₂

## Describe MDH and what is known about the effects of the variation on human biology


Mitochondrial Malate Dehydrogenase, MDH2, is a metabolic enzyme that is important for energy production in humans. Its primary function is within the mitochondrial matrix where the reaction of malate to oxaloacetate occurs using NAD+ and NADH. This reaction is an important component of the citric acid cycle which is an important cycle for the creation of cellular energy (ATP). ₁ From a biochemical standpoint, MDH2 works as a homodimer. A homodimer means two identical protein subunits link together to function. MDH2 contains an N-terminus and a C-terminus. The P133L mutation occurs closer to the N-terminus. ₃ The enzyme uses a specific set of amino acids to hold onto the substrates. In humans, MDH2 is most active in tissues with high metabolic demands. This includes places like the brain, heart, and skeletal muscles. When the protein structure has a mutation at position 133, the enzyme loses its structural stability. This leads to a buildup of lactate as the body tries to find a different way to make energy. This biochemical failure is a direct cause of several things, including early-onset encephalopathy and developmental delays, as the brain is unable to function without the ATP from MDH2. ₄

## Effect of the sequence variant on MDH dynamics
The P133L mutation is predicted to have small but impactful changes within the structure and function of MDH2. When the mutation is compared to the wild type, the overall structural alignment is mostly the same, which suggests little disruption. When looking at areas more closely, such as position 133, you can see there are slight shifts in alignment. If you reference Figure 2.0, you can see the minor changes in alignment. This is most likely due to proline having a more rigid shape, while leucine allows for more flexibility. These small changes can affect the positioning of local loops that are important to the active site. Even though position 133 is not found within the active site, these slight shifts can cause important misalignments in residues within the active site. 
When thinking about dynamics, this mutation is expected to increase flexibility within the 133 region. Increased flexibility can cause the active site to be less stable, which will lower the catalytic efficiency. This could also result in a change in the pKa of nearby residues.

## Description of the data and changes
The aligned structures of the wild type and P133L varient show that the overall structure of the protein is the same with small differences. When looking closer to the mutation site you can see slight shifts in the local structures. This supports the idea that replacing proline with leucine increases flexibility. In the RMSF plots you can see more fluctuation near the 133 residue which further supports this observation. The pKa data also suggests that the mutation affects the local residues. 

## Conclusions and key takeaways
Overall, the P133L mutation in MDH2 causes small structural changes but can still have a large effect on how the enzyme functions. The alligned structures show that the overall shape of the protein stays mostly the same, with local shifts. These changes are mainly due to replacing a rigid proline with a flexable leucine. From the structural allignment and RMSF plots it is clear that flaxability is increased in the area. Even though the site is not within the active site it can still result in changes in the active site. The pKa data also supports this by showing local shifts. What I found most interesting was how my lab group discovered that the P133L varient actually functions more effeciently than the wild type of MDH2. This suggests that the increased flexibility may help the enzyme to work faster and better under certain conditions. This does not mean that the mutation is benefitial overall, but rather more effective from a lab standpoint. Overall, the P133L mutation slightly changes the behavior of MDH2 rather than completly damaging it. These small structural changes are the reason for specific effects on metabolism, which leads to disease, specifically Epileptic Encephalopathy.
